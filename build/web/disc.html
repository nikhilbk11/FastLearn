Foreign Languages

It's sometimes said, even by those who promote eLearning, that some subjects are easier to learn online than others. Usually this is because some complex subjects require a lot of back-and-forth questioning to achieve understanding. When you have to ask three or four questions in a row to zero in on understanding, that's something that takes a minute or two in the classroom, but could take several days on a threaded discussion board. Now, online courses that make use of live chat sessions or teleconferencing can eliminate that difficulty, but such technologies are far from universal in online education, at least so far.

Because of this, one of the subjects that has been considered a poor choice for online study is that of foreign languages. Most schools that specialize in online learning don't even offer courses in foreign languages, even as electives. And that's a shame. Particularly in a world where globalization is advancing, and more and more opportunity is found abroad, the ability to learn a foreign language or two can go a long way toward propelling one's career forward. Even those who are interested only in opportunities in the U.S. would find it useful at least to learn Spanish, which has essentially become the second language of the U.S.

Now, however, the Chronicle of Higher Education is reporting that things may be picking up for learning languages through eLearning. The article lists all sorts of different technologies that help people learn another language, making it seem more surprising than anything else that there's so little emphasis on doing so for credit. And it says that in some cases, students are learning languages online not from a preference, but simply because it's that way or no way. Two cases that highlight this seem like opposites, but have the same effect — when it's difficult to find enough instructors to cover a popular language, it may be easier to find additional instructors to teach online, and when there are only a few students interested in learning a particular language, it may make sense to work with other institutions with the same issue to offer an online course in that language jointly.

eLearners often note that it takes more discipline to thrive in our environment than it does on campus because there's no one there reminding a student at home or work about all the things that are necessary to do. There's only the student remembering to do everything required, and making the time to do all of those things as each week progresses. With foreign language study, this is even more the case. In courses that are based on opinion or interpretation, an intelligent student may sometimes be able to get with putting things together at the last minute. But when it comes to learning a language, nothing will do but practice, practice, practice. Good luck faking that!

So would you consider using eLearning to pick up another language? It might not be the easiest undertaking, whether online or on campus, but the potential rewards are there. Perhaps we indeed will see a noticeable increase in the number of students taking advantage of this opportunity!
